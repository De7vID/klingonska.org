Most commonly adverbials are come at the very beginning of a sentence (though
occasionally an adverbial can be preceded by other stuff, such as a time
element). <small>[TKD 5.4, TKDa 6.7]</small>

<table class="noborder">
  <tr><td>{nom Soppu'}<td><i>They ate quickly.</i>
  <tr><td>{DaHjaj nom Soppu'}<td><i>Today they ate quickly.</i>
</table>

Adverbials are also quite commonly used as exclamations, and then work
sentences in their own right. E.g.: {wej} <i>Not yet! (Don't do it yet!)</i>,
{nom} <i>Come on! Quickly!</i>, {nIteb} <i>Alone! (Do it by
yourself!)</i> <small>[TKD 5.4]</small>

And, finally, if an object is topicalized with the {-'e'} suffix, then the
adverbial can <em>come after</em> the object noun. <small>[TKDa 6.7]</small>

<table class="noborder">
  <tbody>
    <tr><td>{HaqwI' yISam}<td><i>Find the surgeon!</i>
    <tr><td>{DaH HaqwI' yISam}<td><i>Find the surgeon now!</i>
  </tbody>
  <tbody>
    <tr><td>{HaqwI''e' yISam}<td><i>Find the <strong>surgeon</strong>!</i>
    <tr><td>{HaqwI''e' DaH yISam}<td><i>Find the <strong>surgeon</strong> now!</i>
  </tbody>
</table>

There are also two special adverbials ({jay'} <i>intensely</i> and
{neH} <i>only</i>) that, syntactically, work differently than the rest of the
adverbials.<sup><a href="#note1">1</a></sup>

<table>
  <tr>
    <th>Klingon
    <th>English
    <th>Placement
    <th>Source
  <tr>
    <td>{jay'}
    <td><i>intensely (invective)</i>
    <td>At the end of a sentence
    <td><small class="nobr">[TKDa 5.4]</small>
  <tr>
    <td>{neH}
    <td><i>only, merely, just</i>
    <td>Follows a verb <i>or noun</i> it modifies
    <td><small class="nobr">[TKD 5.4, TKW]</small>
</table>

{jay'} is always placed last in the sentence an has the effect of intensifying
it.

<table class="noborder">
  <tr><td>{tIbach}<td><i>Shoot him!</i>
  <tr><td>{tIbach jay'}<td><i>Shoot him, goddamnit!</i>
</table>

When {neH} comes after a verb it has the effect of trivializing it.

<table class="noborder">
  <tbody>
    <tr><td>{vIqIppu'}<td><i>I hit him.</i>
    <tr><td>{vIqIppu' neH}<td><i>I merely hit him.</i>
  </tbody>
  <tbody>
    <tr><td>{Duj yIQotlh}<td><i>Disable the ship!</i>
    <tr><td>{Duj yIQotlh neH}<td><i>Only disable the ship!</i>
  </tbody>
</table>

The use of {neH} in the previous example implies that the ship should be
disabled, but not damaged further.<sup><a href="#note2">2</a></sup>

Alone among the adverbials {neH} can also be used to modify the meaning of a
noun, it then comes after the noun, and means <i>only, alone.</i>

<table class="noborder">
  <tr><td>{yaS neH}<td><i>only the officer, the officer alone</i>
  <tr><td>{jonta' neH}<td><i>just the engine</i>
</table>

Finally adverbials to indicate repetitions are created using a number plus the
suffix {-logh}. This is not limited to the examples in the above list
({wa'logh} <i>once</i> and {cha'logh} <i>twice</i>), but may be used creatively
with any number:

<table class="noborder">
  <tr><td>{vIbachpu'}<td><i>I shot him/her.</i>
  <tr><td>{wa'logh vIbachpu'}<td><i>I shot him/her once.</i>
  <tr><td>{cha'logh vIbachpu'}<td><i>I shot him/her twice.</i>
  <tr><td>{wa'maHlogh vIbachpu'}<td><i>I shot him/her ten times.</i>
  <tr><td>{loS'maH cha'logh vIbachpu'}<td><i>I shot him/her forty two times.</i>
</table>

There are also two adverbials which may be viewed as special cases of {-logh}.
These are {paghlogh} <i>zero times, never</i> (from {pagh} meaning
<i>nothing</i> or <i>zero</i>) and {Hochlogh} <i>all times, always</i> (from
{Hoch} meaning <i>all</i> or <i>every</i>). These mean the same thing as {not}
<i>never</i> and {reH} <i>always</i> but carry extra emphasis.

<table class="noborder">
  <tr>
    <td>{not vIbachpu'}
    <td><i>I never shot him/her.</i>
  <tr>
    <td>{paghlogh vIbachpu'}
    <td><i>I did <strong>never</strong> shoot him/her!</i>
</table>

And that concludes this quick reference to adverbials.

<hr>

<p id=note1><sup><b>1</b></sup> There is also the word {je} (<i>also, and,
too</i>) which, although it is considered a <em>conjunction</em> rather than an
adverbial, it is used in a similar fashion to {neH}. Both {je} and {neH} can be
used after either verbs or nouns (though {je} follow one or more nouns, and
{neH} a single one).</p>

<table class="noborder">
  <tr>
    <td>{qama' vIbach neH}
    <td><i>The only thing I did was to shoot the prisoner.</i>
  <tr>
    <td>{qama' neH vIbach}
    <td><i>The prisoner was the only one I shot.</i>
  <tr>
    <td>{qama' vIbach je}
    <td><i>I shot the prisoner too.</i>
  <tr>
    <td>{qama' 'avwI' je vIbach}
    <td><i>I shot the prisoner and the guard.</i>
</table>

<p id=note2><sup><b>2</b></sup> When {neH} <i>only</i> is used with <i>he</i>
or <i>she</i> as subject, it can be hard to distinguish it from
{neH} <i>want</i>. Extra pronouns or puctuation can then usually be used to
clarify the meaning.</p>

<table class="noborder">
  <tr>
    <td class=nobr>{qama' vIbach vIneH}
    <td><i>I want to shoot the prisoner.</i>
  <tr>
    <td class=nobr>{qama' vIbach neH}
    <td><i>He wants me to shoot the prisoner</i>
      <br>or <i>I only shot the prisoner.</i>
  <tr>
    <td class=nobr>{qama' vIbach neH jIH}
    <td><i>I only shot the prisoner.</i>
  <tr>
    <td class=nobr>{qama' vIbach neH ghaH}
      <br>or {qama' vIbach, neH}
    <td><i>He wants me to shoot the prisoner</i>
</table>
